An exciting new game
for perfect logicians!

Do you have an impecable memory? Can you make all possible inferences in finite time? Our policy based players do!

In this game three opponents battle it out. Each player gets two cards out of a deck of six cards. Every turn, each player must make a valid announcement about their own cards, or the cards of their opponents. The first turn player to know all players' cards wins the game!

Allowed Possible Public Announcements

Even / Odd Number

Agents can make an announcement about the parity of their own, or their opponents cards. Parity announcements can refer to either one or both of a players cards.

Multiple of Three

An announcement similar to the parity announcements mentioned before, however, these announcements refer to players having either a 3 or a 6 in their hand.

One Difference

An announcement that can be made when it is known that the target agent's cards differ by only one point (e.g.: a 3 and a 4).

High / Low Number

An announcement type that refers to the value of the cards of an agent. 1, 2, and 3 count as LOW cards, and 4, 5, and 6 count as HIGH cards.

Know One Card

An announcement that refers to two agents at the same time. It can only be made when there is certainty that one player knows at least on card of another player.

Multiple Policies

Different policies for the participating agents have been implemented in the game, which govern the prefered announcements for the agents.

Kripke model

Observe changes in the Kripke model

After each step in the game, you can choose to observe the current underlying Kripke representation of the model (shown on the right). The graph can be used to observe which worlds each agent still holds for possible, and which announcement could supply them with the information they need.

The vertices in the graphs (colored lines) represent the relations between the worlds. Each vertex is bi-directional, and for each world there is a reflective relation which is not shown. The black circles represent the worlds which are still held possible by at least one agent. Possible worlds which are not held possible by any agent are hidden.

Agent Policies

Random

This agent will make a random announcement of the announcements which it can make.

Choose themselves

An agent with this policy will prefer to make an announcement about their own cards.

Choose other player

This policy will make agents prefer to make announcements that refer to another agent's cards.

Most informative

Makes statements that are the most informative for other players by removing the most possible worlds.

Least informative

Makes statements that are the least informative by removing the least amount of possible worlds.

Policies are crucial

The announcements made in the game can often lead to an opponent instantly winning the game. Therefor, it is important to not give away valuable information that can help your opponents win. We wanted to find out which strategy wins the most games, and we implemented the basic strategies listed above.

Show results

.

Expectations of performance

Considering the nature of the game is aimed towards not giving away information that helps your opponents, we anticipated that the policies "Choose themselves" and "Most informative" will be losing strategies in the game. Additionally, we think that the "Random" policy can randomly give away too much information as well, leading to an opponent winning within the next two turns. We think that the "Least informative" and "Choose other" strategies will be the best performing strategies, with "Least informative" outperforming "Choose other" by a small margin.

We ran an experiment where all possible combinations of policies for 100 games, 10 times per combination, and averaged the win rates for each strategy. During the trials, the starting players were randomized. The results show that "Choose Themselves", and "Most Informative" strategies perform worse than the "Random" strategies (Figure 1 & 5). The "Choose Other", and "Least Informative" performed better than "Random" (Figure 4 & 6). Figure 2 and 3 show how the "Choose Other" and "Least Informative" strategy perform against each other, with different additional opponents.

.

Discussion

It is clear from the results that different strategies in the game have a large influence on the win-rate of the agent. The best strategy turned out to be the "Least Informative" strategy, likely because it prevents other players from winning in the following turns by making announcement that help no-one. The "Choose Other" player is only slightly outperformed by the "Least Informative" strategy. We think this is probably because most possible announcements that can be made about other players are also known by other players, unless they are specifically infered using their own cards. This results in this agent only helping other players sometimes.

All in all, the results are similar to our expectations, although we did not expect the worse performing policies to perform as bad as they did. If we could further investigate the game, we would like to see whether there are even better policies imaginable. We think that the current winning strategy is close to optimal for this game.
